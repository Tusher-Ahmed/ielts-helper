📄 Passage 1 — Vertical Farming (Technology / Environment)

Vertical Farming: Growing Up, Not Out

Vertical farming is an innovative approach to agriculture in which crops are grown in stacked layers, often inside controlled indoor environments such as warehouses or shipping containers. Unlike conventional farming, which spreads outwards across vast fields, vertical farming grows upwards, dramatically reducing the amount of land required. A single vertical farm can produce the same yield as a traditional farm many times its size.

The idea is not entirely new. Although small-scale experiments existed earlier, the modern concept was popularised in 1999 by Dickson Despommier, a professor at Columbia University, who challenged his students to feed a city using only rooftop gardens. When the rooftops proved insufficient, the class began imagining farms that climbed skyward instead.

One of the greatest advantages of vertical farming is its independence from weather. Because crops are grown indoors under artificial conditions, production continues regardless of droughts, floods, or seasonal changes. Temperature, humidity, and light are all carefully regulated, allowing farmers to harvest fresh produce throughout the year. Furthermore, since these farms can be built within cities, food travels a far shorter distance to reach consumers, cutting transport costs and reducing the carbon emissions associated with long-haul delivery.

Water efficiency is another notable benefit. Many vertical farms use hydroponic systems, in which plants grow in nutrient-rich water rather than soil. These systems recycle water continuously, using up to 95 per cent less than traditional irrigation. Pesticides are also largely unnecessary, as the enclosed environment keeps most pests out.

However, vertical farming is not without its critics. The most significant drawback is energy consumption. Replacing natural sunlight with artificial LED lighting requires enormous amounts of electricity, and unless that power comes from renewable sources, the environmental gains may be offset. The high initial cost of constructing and equipping a vertical farm also remains a barrier, putting it beyond the reach of many small businesses.

Despite these challenges, interest in the technology continues to grow. Several countries with limited farmland, such as Singapore and the United Arab Emirates, have invested heavily in vertical farms to strengthen their food security. As technology improves and energy becomes cleaner, supporters believe vertical farming could play a vital role in feeding the world's rapidly expanding urban population.

📄 Passage 2 — The Power of Sleep (Health) — খুলে প্র্যাকটিস করো

The Power of a Good Night's Sleep

For much of human history, sleep was regarded as a passive state — a simple switching-off of the body at the end of the day. Modern science, however, has revealed that sleep is one of the most active and essential processes for human health. Far from being wasted time, the hours we spend asleep allow the brain and body to repair, reorganise, and prepare for the day ahead.

During sleep, the brain cycles through several distinct stages. The deepest stage, known as slow-wave sleep, is when physical restoration takes place: tissues are repaired, muscles grow, and the immune system is strengthened. Later in the night, a stage called REM (rapid eye movement) sleep becomes more frequent. It is during REM sleep that most dreaming occurs, and researchers believe this phase is crucial for consolidating memories and processing emotions.

A lack of sufficient sleep can have serious consequences. Studies have consistently linked chronic sleep deprivation to a weakened immune system, weight gain, and an increased risk of heart disease. The effects on the mind are equally striking. People who sleep poorly often struggle with concentration, decision-making, and mood regulation. In fact, staying awake for twenty-four hours can impair performance to a degree comparable with being legally drunk.

So how much sleep do we actually need? While individual requirements vary, most adults function best with between seven and nine hours per night. Teenagers generally need more, owing to the demands of a developing brain. Yet in modern society, sleep is frequently sacrificed in favour of work, study, or entertainment, and many people consistently fall short.

Fortunately, the quality of sleep can be improved through simple habits. Experts recommend keeping a regular schedule, avoiding screens before bed, and limiting caffeine in the afternoon. A cool, dark, and quiet bedroom also promotes deeper rest. By treating sleep as a priority rather than a luxury, individuals can protect both their physical and mental wellbeing.
